Overview

This is an introductory course in statistics for students specializing in the life sciences. The emphasis is on statistical ideas rather than computations or mathematical formulations. There are no prerequisites beyond elementary algebra.

In this course, students will:

  1. Learn to identify key statistical issues to be addressed during the design phase of an experiment (e.g., threats to valid conclusions, identifying confounders, appropriate measurements, and randomization).

  2. Become proficient with intermediate statistical methods: comparing groups (analysis of variance), analyzing associations (linear regression), and methods for categorical data (contingency tables and odds ratios). You should know which methods are useful in different situations and which conditions must be checked for a method to be applicable.

  3. Be able to perform detailed data analyses on a variety of biological datasets using the statistical computing environment R, including basic programming. You should be able to implement all the methods discussed in this course.

  2. Lecture slides. Lecture slides are the primary source of information for the course. They will be made available online after lecture.

  3. Textbooks. There is no required textbook; however, the material is based on the following:

    • Statistics for the Life Sciences (5th ed.) by Samuels, Witmer, and Schaffner
    • The Analysis of Biological Data (3rd ed.) by Whitlock and Schluter
    • An Introduction to Statistical Learning with Applications in R by James et al. (2021)
    • Modern Statistics for Modern Biology by Susan Holmes and Wolfgang Huber (online version)

The Honor Code

Instructors and students are expected to follow Stanford’s Honor Code in all matters relating to this course. You are encouraged to meet and exchange ideas with your classmates while studying and working on homework assignments, but you are individually responsible for your own work and for understanding the material. You are not permitted to copy or otherwise reference another student’s homework or computer code. If you have any questions regarding this policy, feel free to contact the instructor.

Compromising your academic integrity may lead to serious consequences, including (but not limited to) one or more of the following: failure of the assignment, failure of the course, disciplinary probation, suspension from the university, or dismissal from the university.

Students are responsible for understanding the University’s Honor Code policy and must make proper use of citations of sources when writing papers, creating and presenting work, taking examinations, and doing research.
